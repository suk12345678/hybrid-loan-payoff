The Snowball Method Explained

Build momentum with quick wins

How It Works

The snowball method is simple:

  1. Make minimum payments on all your loans
  2. Put any extra money toward the loan with the smallest balance
  3. Once that loan is paid off, move to the next-smallest balance
  4. Repeat until all loans are paid off

Why It's Called "Snowball"

Like a snowball rolling downhill, you start small and build momentum. Each paid-off loan frees up money to attack the next one, and your progress accelerates.

The Psychology Behind It

Paying off debt is hard. It takes years. It's easy to lose motivation.

The snowball method gives you frequent wins. Each time you eliminate a loan, you get a psychological boost that keeps you going.

Example

You have three loans and $500/month to put toward them:

  • Loan A: $10,000 at 7% (minimum payment: $116)
  • Loan B: $8,000 at 5% (minimum payment: $85)
  • Loan C: $5,000 at 4% (minimum payment: $46)

Snowball approach:

  • Pay minimums on A and B ($201 total)
  • Put remaining $299 toward C (smallest balance)
  • Once C is paid off (~17 months), attack B, then A

Result: First loan paid off in 17 months, all loans paid off in ~4.7 years, ~$3,100 in total interest

Pros of the Snowball Method

1. Quick Wins Build Momentum

Seeing a loan balance hit $0 is incredibly motivating. It proves you can do this.

2. Simplifies Your Life

Every loan you eliminate is one less payment to track, one less account to manage.

3. Reduces Minimum Payments

Each paid-off loan frees up its minimum payment, giving you more flexibility if money gets tight.

4. Works with Human Psychology

We're not robots. We need encouragement. Snowball provides it.

Cons of the Snowball Method

1. Costs More in Interest

By ignoring interest rates, you'll pay more over the life of your loans compared to avalanche.

2. Takes Slightly Longer

Because you're not prioritizing high-interest debt, it takes a bit longer to pay everything off.

3. Requires Ignoring the Math

If you're someone who obsesses over optimizing every dollar, snowball might frustrate you.

When to Use the Snowball Method

You Need Motivation

If you've tried paying off debt before and quit, snowball's quick wins can keep you engaged.

You Have Multiple Small Loans

If you have several loans under $3,000, knocking them out quickly simplifies your financial life.

Your Interest Rates Are Similar

If all your loans are within 1-2% of each other, the interest savings from avalanche are minimal. Snowball's psychological benefits outweigh the small cost difference.

You Have Variable Income

Eliminating loans reduces your minimum monthly payment, giving you breathing room during lean months.

When NOT to Use the Snowball Method

You Have High-Interest Debt

If you have loans at 8%+ interest, the cost of ignoring them can be significant. Avalanche or hybrid might be better.

You're Highly Disciplined

If you don't need psychological wins to stay motivated, avalanche will save you more money.

Your Smallest Loan Is Tiny

If your smallest loan is $500 and your next-smallest is $15,000, snowball won't give you momentum for long.

Snowball vs Avalanche: The Numbers

Using the same example from earlier:

Comparison

Snowball:

  • Time to payoff: ~4.7 years
  • Total interest: ~$3,100
  • First loan paid off: 17 months

Avalanche:

  • Time to payoff: ~4.5 years
  • Total interest: ~$2,800
  • First loan paid off: ~27 months

Difference: Snowball costs ~$300 more but gives you a win 10 months sooner

Is that worth it? Depends on what keeps you motivated.

How to Implement Snowball

Step 1: List Your Loans by Balance

Smallest to largest. This is your payoff order.

Step 2: Calculate Your Extra Payment

Total monthly budget for loans minus total minimum payments = extra payment amount.

Step 3: Automate It

Set up automatic payments for minimums on all loans, then manually add extra to the smallest loan each month.

Step 4: Celebrate Each Win

When you pay off a loan, acknowledge it. Then immediately redirect that payment to the next loan.

The "Snowball Effect" in Action

Here's why it's called a snowball:

  • Month 1-17: Pay $299 extra on Loan C
  • Loan C paid off! Now you have $299 + $46 (C's old minimum) = $345 extra for Loan B
  • Loan B paid off! Now you have $345 + $85 (B's old minimum) = $430 extra for Loan A
  • Your final loan gets crushed with massive payments
